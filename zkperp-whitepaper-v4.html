Abstract

ZKPerp is a decentralized perpetual futures exchange built natively on the Aleo blockchain — a Layer-1 network designed from the ground up around zero-knowledge proofs. Unlike existing perpetual DEX protocols (GMX, dYdX, Hyperliquid), where every position size, entry price, leverage ratio, and PnL figure is permanently visible on-chain, ZKPerp stores all position data in Aleo private records: encrypted UTXO-style objects readable only by the position owner's viewing key.

This is not a privacy layer bolted on top of a transparent system. ZKPerp is built in Leo, Aleo's native language, and leverages the platform's fundamental property: the Aleo VM can verify the mathematical correctness of any computation — that a position was opened at a valid price, with valid collateral, producing a valid PnL — entirely through a ZK proof, without the contract ever learning the underlying values.

This privacy guarantee creates a genuine engineering challenge: how do you liquidate a position you cannot see? How do you reserve pool liquidity against unrealised losses you cannot compute? How do you relay reliable prices from off-chain feeds without a single point of trust? This whitepaper describes the solutions ZKPerp has built and deployed on Aleo testnet to answer each of these questions.

The Privacy Problem in DeFi Trading

Every perpetual DEX deployed on an EVM chain exposes all trading activity in full. Position opens, closes, liquidations, and LP deposits are written to public storage and indexed by dozens of data providers within seconds. This is not a minor inconvenience — it is a structural attack surface that fundamentally limits who can trade and on what terms.

Concrete Attack Vectors

Front-Running
  • A whale opens a $50M long on ETH. Bots see the pending tx in the mempool.
  • MEV bots buy ETH before the tx lands, then sell immediately after — pocketing the price impact at the whale's expense.
  • On GMX this is partially mitigated by execution price limits, but the position itself is instantly public once settled.
Liquidation Hunting
  • Every open position's liquidation price is calculable from public data: entry price, collateral, and size are all on-chain.
  • Coordinated traders push price toward known liquidation clusters to trigger cascading liquidations and earn rewards.
  • This is documented behaviour on GMX, dYdX, and Hyperliquid.
Strategy Copying
  • Every trade by a skilled trader is visible the moment it settles. Competitors can replicate positions in real-time.
  • Copy-trading platforms are built entirely on this transparency — by design in retail, but a leak for professionals.
  • Institutions cannot trade meaningful size without telegraphing their thesis to the entire market.

Why Existing Solutions Are Insufficient

Several projects have attempted to add privacy to EVM perpetual trading. The approaches fall into two categories, both with fundamental weaknesses:

  • Encrypted mempools / commit-reveal schemes — hide order intent briefly during submission, but position data is public once settled. Liquidation hunting still works; strategy copying still works.
  • ZK proof layers on EVM — require the underlying EVM state to remain transparent (for EVM compatibility). Privacy is optional and per-transaction, not a system default. Position history leaks across time.

The Aleo Difference

Aleo's programming model distinguishes between public state (mappings: readable by anyone, stored on-chain) and private records (UTXO-style encrypted objects, readable only by the owner's viewing key). ZKPerp stores all position data in private records — privacy is the default, not an option.

Fundamental Property

In Aleo, a smart contract transition can verify that an operation is mathematically correct — that a position was opened at a valid price with valid collateral — entirely via ZK proof, without the contract ever learning the actual numbers. The verifier learns only that the computation was correct, not what it computed over. This is not straightforward to replicate on EVM-compatible chains, where all contract state is inherently public and privacy requires additional layers that do not share this native property.

What Remains Public in ZKPerp

For full transparency, the following information is public on-chain in ZKPerp even with its privacy model:

  • Oracle prices — required for correct settlement, unavoidably public
  • Pool state — total liquidity, total LP tokens, aggregate long OI, aggregate short OI
  • Order existence — a boolean in pending_orders indicating some order is active, but not its trigger price or direction
  • Transaction counts — the number of positions opened/closed per block (not their contents)

Everything else — position size, entry price, collateral, leverage, direction, PnL, and liquidation level — is encrypted.

Leo Language Notes

Leo is a statically-typed, ZK-native language that compiles to Aleo instructions. Two quirks are significant for ZKPerp's implementation:

  • Ternary both-branch evaluation — Leo evaluates both branches of a ternary before selecting one. This means a > b ? a - b : 0u64 will overflow if a < b, even though the false branch would be selected. All subtraction in ZKPerp uses the safe-subtraction pattern: cap first, then subtract.
  • 32-call-per-transaction limit — Aleo limits the number of program calls in a single transaction. This caps bulk operations (e.g. bulk aid distribution in HumanityLink) at 16 recipients per tx, with batching at the orchestrator layer.
// ❌ UNSAFE — Leo evaluates a - b even when a <= b
let result: u64 = a > b ? a - b : 0u64;

// ✅ SAFE — cap first, then subtract (always valid)
let capped_b: u64 = b <= a ? b : a;
let result: u64 = a - capped_b;

Slot Record Model

Aleo's UTXO record model creates a unique UX problem: every blockchain operation that produces output creates a new record in the wallet. Without mitigation, a trader who makes N round-trip trades accumulates 2N records — one PositionSlot and one LiquidationAuth per trade. The wallet has no reliable way to know which records are spent without querying the chain, so on every session load it must request decryption of all N records, triggering N Shield Wallet approval popups. At 10 trades this is annoying; at 50 trades it is completely unusable.

Root Cause: UTXO Accumulation

Aleo records are consumed and reissued — they cannot be modified in place. Every open_position creates two new records (PositionSlot + LiquidationAuth). Every close_position consumes the PositionSlot but returns nothing, leaving the wallet to infer from the closed_positions mapping that the record is spent. The wallet's record scanner sees a growing list of ciphertexts and must decrypt all of them to find the live ones.

Solution: Persistent Fixed-Count Slots (v12+)

ZKPerp v12 introduced the slot model: each trader initialises exactly three records once, which persist for the lifetime of their account. The record count never grows.

Records Issued at Initialization

  • PositionSlot (slot_id: 0) — Long position slot
  • PositionSlot (slot_id: 1) — Short position slot
  • LPSlot — Liquidity provider position, always merged into existing record

Invariant Maintained

Every transition that touches a slot is exactly one slot in, one slot out. The record is technically new each time (Aleo's UTXO model requires this) but the count stays constant. After 1000 trades, the wallet holds exactly 3 records — the same as day one.

Slot Constraint and Enforcement

A slot can only hold one position at a time. The Leo contract asserts !slot.is_open inside open_position and place_limit_order — if the slot is already open, the transaction reverts. A long slot (slot_id 0) cannot hold two simultaneous long positions. This is a deliberate design constraint that trades multi-position complexity for the UX guarantee of fixed record count.

Spent Record Detection

Before v12, the frontend used localStorage to track which records were spent — fragile and browser-dependent. v12 replaced this with the closed_positions on-chain mapping: a field => bool that is set when a position is closed or liquidated. The frontend now pre-filters records by querying this mapping before requesting wallet decryption, reducing popups to only genuinely active records.

UX Result

After 1,000 trades, the wallet holds 3 records. Every session requires clicking Unshield All Private Info once — one Shield Wallet approval — to decrypt all 3 slots simultaneously. The UX is identical on day one and year five.

Onboarding Flow

  1. New trader visits Trade page → sees Initialize Account modal
  2. Single wallet approval → initialize_slots() mints 3 slot records on-chain
  3. On next visit: click 🛡 Unshield All Private Info → one approval decrypts all slots
  4. USDCx balance, positions, and LP amount are now visible in the UI
  5. Trading and LP operations pass the slot plaintext as the first argument to each transition
  6. Every subsequent session: same 3 records, same single approval

LP Pool Design & Withdrawal Guard

The ZKPerp liquidity pool is a single-sided USDC pool. LPs deposit USDCx and receive proportional LP tokens. The pool acts as the direct counterparty to all traders — when a trader profits, the pool pays; when a trader loses, the pool earns. There is no impermanent loss from token price divergence because the pool holds only one asset.

LP Token Minting and Redemption

LP tokens are minted on deposit and burned on withdrawal using a standard proportional formula with a guard against first-deposit division by zero:

// Minting
lp_to_mint = deposit_amount × (total_lp_tokens + 1) / (total_liquidity + 1)

// Redemption
usdc_out = amount_to_burn × total_liquidity / (total_lp_tokens + 1)

For the first LP depositor, lp_to_mint ≈ deposit_amount, establishing 1:1 token-to-USDC parity at inception. Subsequent depositors receive slightly fewer LP tokens per USDC if the pool has grown from accumulated fees or trader losses — the standard AMM dilution protection.

The Withdrawal Guard — Full Formula

This is the core LP protection mechanism. Available withdrawable liquidity is not simply "my pro-rata share of the pool". It must account for four components, all enforced on-chain inside finalize_remove_liquidity:

available = total_liquidity
          − long_open_interest      // full notional locked, not netted
          − short_open_interest     // full notional locked, not netted
          − pnl_liability           // max(net_unrealised_pnl, 0)
          − safety_buffer           // 10% × total_liquidity

A withdrawal that would leave the pool below this threshold is rejected by the Leo contract — the transaction reverts with a failed assertion. The LP's tokens are not consumed.

Asymmetric PnL Treatment

The most important design decision in the pool: unrealised PnL is treated asymmetrically. This mirrors the approach in GMX V2 and is the correct conservative design.

Trader StatePool TreatmentLP Withdrawal Capacity
Traders up +10%Real liability — reserved as pnl_liabilityDecreases
Traders flatZero liabilityUnchanged (baseline)
Traders down −10%Floored at zero — not a real asset yetUnchanged — not increased
Position closed at profitRealised — pool pays USDC to traderDecreases by payout amount
Position closed at lossRealised — pool earns collateralIncreases as total_liquidity grows

When traders are losing, the pool holds paper gains — but these are not yet a real asset. Prices can recover before positions close. If LPs could withdraw based on paper gains and the market then reversed, the pool could become undercollateralised. The floor at zero prevents this:

let pnl_liability: u64 = net_pnl > 0i64 ? net_pnl as u64 : 0u64;

Why Long OI and Short OI Are Not Netted

Both sides are locked independently and summed — they do not net off against each other. A book with $5M long and $5M short appears balanced on paper, but in a correlated market shock both sides can move simultaneously against the pool. A sudden 30% ETH drop liquidates longs and potentially forces the pool to cover both sides' losses. Netting would dramatically understate the true capital requirement. The full long_oi + short_oi lock is the conservative, correct approach — adopted by all professional perp DEX designs.

The 10% Safety Buffer

The buffer (WITHDRAWAL_BUFFER_BPS = 100_000 out of 1,000,000) serves three purposes:

  • Oracle lag — the on-chain price may be up to one oracle tick behind the real market. The buffer absorbs the gap between a price move and the next oracle update.
  • Liquidation shortfall — if a position is liquidated but the trader's collateral is insufficient to cover the loss (extreme price spike), the pool absorbs the deficit. The buffer is the first line of defence.
  • Withdrawal race conditions — if multiple LPs attempt to withdraw simultaneously near the limit, the buffer prevents a race where the last withdrawal leaves the pool insolvent.

Trusted PnL Aggregation

Because position data is stored in private Aleo records, the Leo contract cannot compute unrealised PnL on-chain by itself — it cannot read the position data. The orchestrator bot holds LiquidationAuth records for every open position (these contain the same position fields) and computes net PnL off-chain on each oracle tick:

// Orchestrator off-chain computation (Node.js)
net_pnl = Σ positions {
  if is_long:  pnl_i = size × (current_price − entry_price) / entry_price
  if is_short: pnl_i = size × (entry_price − current_price) / entry_price
}

// Submitted on-chain
await updateNetPnl(net_pnl)  // calls update_net_pnl(net_pnl: i64) on Leo contract
Known Limitation — Trusted Aggregator

The on-chain contract can verify only that the caller is the registered orchestrator (assert_eq(caller, orchestrator)). It cannot verify the mathematical correctness of the submitted net_pnl. This is documented honestly. The failure mode is safe: if the orchestrator is offline and never submits PnL, Mapping::get_or_use(0i64) defaults to zero, enforcing the conservative baseline (full OI + safety buffer) — the pool cannot be over-withdrawn. The path to trustless PnL is a ZK proof over LiquidationAuth records — a future research direction.

Liquidation Architecture

Liquidation is the hardest unsolved problem in any privacy-preserving perpetual DEX. The fundamental conflict: positions are private records readable only by their owner, yet a liquidator must read the position to assess its health and must pass the record into the Leo transition to consume it. Solving this without compromising trader privacy requires a carefully designed dual-record architecture.

Why View Keys Are Not Enough

An Aleo view key lets the holder decrypt and read records — but not spend them. Passing a record into a Leo transition requires consuming it on-chain, which requires the record owner's private key. Even if the orchestrator could read the trader's position via view key, it could not pass that record into liquidate() — the transaction would be invalid because the orchestrator does not own the record.

ActionWho CallsOwns the Record?Works?
close_position(slot)TraderYes — it's their slot✅ Yes
liquidate(slot) (view key only)OrchestratorNo — trader owns slot❌ No
liquidate(liq_auth) (dual record)OrchestratorYes — orchestrator owns LiquidationAuth✅ Yes

Dual-Record Architecture

Every open_position() call creates exactly two private records simultaneously in a single atomic transaction:

The orchestrator is a single registered wallet address stored on-chain in the roles mapping. It owns the LiquidationAuth record — a mirror of the position data — and can pass it into liquidate() without ever touching or seeing the trader's PositionSlot.

Liquidation Formula

// Margin remaining at current oracle price
pnl = is_long
  ? size × (oracle_price − entry_price) / entry_price
  : size × (entry_price − oracle_price) / entry_price

remaining_margin = collateral + pnl  // pnl may be negative

// Liquidation condition (verified on-chain in finalize_liquidate)
margin_ratio = remaining_margin / size × 100
assert(margin_ratio < 1)  // reverts if position is healthy

Liquidation Flow End-to-End

  1. Orchestrator bot polls Provable Record Scanner API for LiquidationAuth records owned by the orchestrator wallet
  2. For each record, bot computes margin_ratio using the current on-chain oracle price
  3. If margin_ratio < 1%, bot submits liquidate(liq_auth) transaction
  4. finalize_liquidate re-verifies the margin condition against the live oracle mapping — the bot cannot fabricate or front-run this check
  5. On success: LiquidationAuth consumed; trader's position mapping entry cleared; fresh empty PositionSlot atomically returned to trader's address; 0.5% reward paid to liquidator; remaining collateral returned to LP pool
  6. Trader's wallet count is unchanged: they still hold 3 records (now with empty position slots)

Replay and Double-Execution Prevention

The active_position_ids mapping stores a field => bool for every live position ID. Both close_position and liquidate assert that the position ID is active before processing, then clear it atomically. This prevents:

  • Double-liquidation — a position that was already liquidated cannot be liquidated again, even if a stale LiquidationAuth record is replayed
  • Close + liquidate race — if a trader closes their position and a liquidation tx lands in the same block, only one will succeed; the other will revert on the mapping check
  • Stale TP/SL execution — execute_take_profit and execute_stop_loss also check active_position_ids, preventing execution of orders for positions that were already closed or liquidated

Advanced Order Types

ZKPerp supports three advanced order types beyond spot market open/close. All follow the same keeper-executed pattern: the trader submits order parameters, the orchestrator receives a PendingOrder private record, monitors the oracle, and submits execution when the trigger condition is met on-chain. The trader never needs to be online after placing an order.

Privacy Model for Orders

Order trigger prices, sizes, and directions are never published on-chain before execution. The only on-chain footprint of a pending order is a single boolean in the pending_orders mapping, keyed by a BHP256 commitment hash:

struct OrderIdInput {
  trader: address,
  nonce: field,
  trigger_price: u64,
}
order_id = BHP256::hash_to_field(OrderIdInput { trader, nonce, trigger_price })

// On-chain: only this is stored
pending_orders: field => bool   // true = order active, null = executed/cancelled

An observer can see that some order exists, but cannot determine the trigger price, direction, or size from the commitment hash. The PendingOrder record itself is sent to the orchestrator as a private record — only the orchestrator can read or consume it.

Order Flows

TP vs SL Execution Price — Why They Differ

Take Profit fills at the trigger price (limit semantics): the trader sets a target and gets exactly that price regardless of where the oracle is at the moment of execution. Stop Loss fills at the oracle price at execution time (market semantics): the trigger activates the close, but the actual fill is at whatever price the keeper submits the transaction at. Speed of execution matters more than exact price when stopping losses.

Take Profit (Long, entry $95k, TP $100k)

Oracle at execution: $104,500
PnL calculated at: $100,000 (trigger)
pnl = 5M × (100k − 95k) / 95k = +263,157
payout = collateral + pnl = 1,258,157

Trader gets exactly the price they set. Oracle overshoot is irrelevant.

Stop Loss (Long, entry $95k, SL $92k)

Oracle at execution: $90,000
PnL calculated at: $90,000 (market oracle)
pnl = 5M × (90k − 95k) / 95k = −263,157
payout = collateral + pnl = 731,843

SL trigger fired at $92k but filled at $90k — slippage risk in fast markets.

Security: Keeper Cannot Fabricate Execution

The oracle price condition is verified inside finalize_execute_* using the live on-chain oracle mapping — not a value supplied by the keeper. The keeper cannot claim a trigger was met when it wasn't; the Aleo finalize layer will revert if the oracle price does not satisfy the condition at the time the transaction is processed.

Oracle Infrastructure — 2-of-3 Chainlink Relay

ZKPerp requires tamper-resistant price feeds for BTC, ETH, and SOL. Chainlink and Pyth do not natively support Aleo. Rather than using a single centralised price bot (a single point of manipulation), ZKPerp operates a live 2-of-3 signed quorum relay that bridges Chainlink's institutional-grade EVM price feeds to the Aleo blockchain.

Live on Testnet

aleo-oracle — a 3-relayer Chainlink relay with coordinator quorum — is deployed on Render and actively feeding prices to all three ZKPerp contracts. This is not a planned feature; it is operational today.

The 1-hour heartbeat is a maximum interval; Chainlink updates immediately on >0.5% price deviation. Polling every 15 seconds means ZKPerp catches price moves within 15 seconds of Chainlink publishing them.

Quorum Protocol Detail

Each relayer independently reads its assigned Chainlink feeds and signs a canonical payload using a dedicated secp256k1 key — completely separate from any Aleo wallet key. The coordinator maintains an allowlist of the three relayer Ethereum addresses and rejects submissions from any unknown source. When 2-of-3 relayers agree on the identical 4-tuple (assetId, price, roundId, updatedAt), the quorum fires.

What Prevents a False Price?

  • All 3 relayers read directly from Chainlink — they have no mechanism to inject arbitrary prices independently of the feed
  • 2-of-3 must agree on the identical payload — a single rogue relayer is outvoted
  • The coordinator checks relayer addresses against its allowlist
  • The bot applies a 1% deviation guard before submitting to Aleo
  • The Leo contract asserts caller == ORACLE_ADMIN

Failure Modes

  • 1 relayer offline — 2 remaining reach quorum, no impact
  • 2 relayers offline — quorum fails, no price update; Binance fallback activates for BTC only
  • Coordinator offline — no price updates; existing on-chain price used; withdrawal guard conservative baseline applies
  • All 3 compromised — false price possible; mitigated by key separation and monitoring

Key Design Decisions

  • 1% deviation guard (bot-side) — the bot only submits an Aleo transaction if the new price differs ≥1% from the current on-chain value. This keeps testnet transaction fees manageable. The 1% threshold is a configuration parameter.
  • Sequential fan-out, 3s gap — BTC, ETH, and SOL updates are submitted one at a time with a 3-second interval to avoid hitting Provable API rate limits on the Aleo submission layer.
  • Deduplication on (assetId, roundId, updatedAt) — the coordinator will not resubmit the same Chainlink round twice, even after a restart. This prevents duplicate Aleo transactions.
  • Key separation — relayer secp256k1 keys are used only for coordinator authentication. A stolen relayer key cannot submit Aleo transactions directly — it is not an Aleo private key.
  • Binance fallback (BTC only) — if the oracle relay is offline, the bot falls back to Binance/CoinGecko spot price for BTC, maintaining basic liquidation functionality for the largest market.

Adding New Markets

The relay is configurable via config/markets.json. Adding a new market (e.g. AVAX/USD) requires:

  1. Add the Chainlink feed address and chain to markets.json
  2. Deploy a new Leo program (e.g. zkperp_avax_v21.aleo)
  3. Set PROGRAM_ID_AVAX=zkperp_avax_v21.aleo in the bot environment
  4. Extend CONFIG.programs routing in zkperp-bot.mjs

No changes are needed to the coordinator or relayer code — the asset routing is handled entirely in the bot layer.

Path to Full On-Chain Verification

The current relay reduces trust surface compared to a single oracle bot — a single compromised relayer cannot influence the price — but it remains a federated, off-chain coordination system. The quorum result is forwarded to the bot, which the Leo contract trusts by role check (assert_eq(caller, ORACLE_ADMIN)), not by cryptographic proof.

One exploratory direction for stronger guarantees would be to have the Leo contract verify the 3 secp256k1 signatures directly inside finalize_update_price. If feasible within Aleo's constraint system, this would make the quorum check part of the ZK proof rather than an off-chain coordination step — meaning the coordinator's honesty would no longer be a trust assumption. This is not a near-term roadmap item; secp256k1 verification inside a Leo finalize is a non-trivial research and engineering problem.

ZK Dark Pool

ZKDarkPool (zkdarkpool_v2.aleo) is a companion protocol to ZKPerp, deployed and verified on Aleo testnet. It implements institutional-grade batch auction settlement where all bids and asks are sealed until a uniform clearing price is computed and proven. The first live on-chain settlement matched a BTC buy/sell order at a clearing price of $49,500.

The Problem with Continuous Order Books

In a continuous limit order book (CLOB), order placement and cancellation are public events. Large institutional orders must be broken up into small slices to avoid telegraphing intent — a well-understood practice called "iceberg orders" in traditional finance. Even sliced orders are visible in the order flow data after the fact. MEV bots can detect large buy-side pressure building up and front-run each slice. The result: institutional traders pay significant implicit costs that retail liquidity ultimately absorbs.

Batch Auction Mechanics

A batch auction sidesteps the continuous book entirely. All orders submitted during a fixed interval are settled simultaneously at a single price:

Key Properties

  • Zero price impact for large orders — all orders at the clearing price receive the same execution, regardless of size or submission time within the window
  • Time priority eliminated — submitting 1 second before window close is as good as submitting 59 seconds before. This removes the incentive for latency arms races.
  • No information leakage pre-settlement — only the buyer and seller know their own order details. The matcher decrypts only its own records (as the orchestrator), not other participants'.
  • Permissionless settlement — anyone who can construct a valid proof of a matched pair can call settle_match(), not just the operator

  • Leo 3.5.0 / GLIBC incompatibility — Leo 3.5.0 would not run on the Render Ubuntu instance. Resolved by building Leo 3.4.0 from source in the deployment environment.
  • Validator gossip race conditions — settle_match() transactions were failing with HTTP 500 on local devnet due to node gossip timing. Resolved by moving settlement to public testnet where block confirmation is reliable.
  • Collateral overflow in u64 — fill_size × clearing_price overflows u64 at realistic market prices. Resolved by using u128 for intermediate multiplication before dividing back to u64.

USDCx & Cross-Chain Bridge

ZKPerp uses USDCx (test_usdcx_stablecoin.aleo) — the official Aleo testnet stablecoin — as its collateral and settlement token. USDCx is Aleo's native USDC equivalent, issued by Provable and integrated with real cross-chain bridging infrastructure.

Integration Points

  • LP deposits — call transfer_from_public(trader → zkperp_btc_v21.aleo) to move USDCx into the pool
  • Position collateral — transferred to the contract address at open_position time
  • Position payouts — transferred from pool to trader on close_position via transfer_public
  • Liquidation rewards — 0.5% of position size paid to the liquidator address in USDCx
  • Limit order margin — held by self.address (the Leo program) during the pending period, not by the orchestrator

Obtaining USDCx — ETH → Aleo Bridge

Traders obtain USDCx by bridging USDC from Ethereum Sepolia testnet to Aleo. ZKPerp's frontend provides a guided bridge flow:

The "Bridge USDCx →" button in the ZKPerp frontend links directly to the bridge UI and provides step-by-step guidance. Live test: ~81 USDC bridged successfully to Aleo testnet in under 15 minutes.

USDCx in Transactions

Because test_usdcx_stablecoin.aleo is a separate program, all ZKPerp transitions that move USDCx emit a cross-program call which is returned as a Future and awaited in the Leo finalize layer. This pattern ensures atomicity: if the token transfer fails for any reason, the entire position operation reverts.

// Example: open_position atomically locks collateral
async transition open_position(
  slot: PositionSlot,
  public collateral: u64,
  ...
) -> (PositionSlot, LiquidationAuth, Future) {
  let f: Future = test_usdcx_stablecoin.aleo/transfer_from_public(
    self.caller, self.address, collateral
  );
  return (new_slot, liq_auth, finalize_open_position(f, ...));
}

async function finalize_open_position(transfer_future: Future, ...) {
  transfer_future.await();  // reverts if USDC transfer failed
  // ... update pool state mappings
}

Known Limitations

ZKPerp is testnet software. The following limitations are documented in full as part of the protocol's commitment to transparency.

Resolved — USDCx Token Integration
Fully integrated with test_usdcx_stablecoin.aleo. All collateral flows use real token transfers. ETH → Aleo bridge via xReserve tested live.
Resolved — Record Accumulation (Slot Model)
v12 slot model: wallet holds exactly 3 records regardless of trading history. Introduced in Wave 3, fully deployed.
Resolved — Liquidation Mechanism
Dual-record (Option D) architecture fully implemented and live. Automated keeper bot handles all liquidations. Manual liquidation also possible by anyone.
Resolved — Single Oracle (Single Point of Failure)
2-of-3 Chainlink relay now live on testnet. Three independent relayers must agree before a price update fires.
Resolved — Maximum Privacy Contract (v21)
v21 strips all position parameters from finalize functions. Only hashes and nonces are exposed on-chain. Business logic (leverage, slippage, margin) is proven entirely in the ZK transition. Pool state is updated via orchestrator-only update_pool_state calls.
Open — Trusted PnL Aggregation
Net unrealised PnL submitted by orchestrator is not cryptographically verified on-chain. Failure mode is safe (defaults to zero = conservative baseline). Trustless ZK PnL proof is a future research direction.
Open — Single Orchestrator
One wallet controls liquidations and order execution. If offline, no automatic actions fire (traders can still close manually). Multi-orchestrator rotation is planned.
Open — No Funding Rate
Current fee model is a flat borrow rate per block. Dynamic funding rates (GMX/Perp style: longs pay shorts when long OI > short OI) are planned for mainnet. Without funding rates, prolonged OI imbalance costs LPs.
Resolved — Cancel Order Transitions
cancel_tp_sl and cancel_limit_order are live in v21. Traders can cancel any active TP, SL, or limit order by presenting their PositionSlot and OrderReceipt. No orchestrator involvement required.
Open — No Partial Close
Positions must be closed in full. Partial close (reduce size by X%) is architecturally supported by the slot model and will be added in a future Leo version.
Language — Leo Ternary Both-Branch Evaluation
Leo evaluates both branches of ternary operators before selecting. All subtraction operations use safe-subtraction patterns throughout the codebase to prevent arithmetic underflow.
Language — 32-Call-Per-Transaction Limit
Aleo's VM caps cross-program calls per transaction at 32, which limits bulk operations to ~16 recipients in a single tx. Batching is handled at the orchestrator/PostgreSQL layer.

What the Keeper Bot Cannot Do

  • Liquidate a healthy position — margin condition verified on-chain against live oracle
  • Execute a TP/SL when trigger not met — oracle condition verified in finalize layer
  • Execute an order for a closed/liquidated position — active_position_ids check prevents this
  • Steal locked margin from limit orders — margin held by the Leo program address, not the orchestrator key
  • Read the trader's PositionSlot — the orchestrator does not own this record; it cannot decrypt it

Privacy Guarantees

  • Position size, entry price, leverage, PnL, liquidation level — encrypted in Aleo private records; readable only by the position owner's viewing key
  • Order trigger prices — sealed behind BHP256 commitment hashes; never published before execution
  • LP balances — stored in private LP slot records; only the LP can see their own position
  • Orchestrator's knowledge — limited to the data in LiquidationAuth and PendingOrder records it owns. It cannot access the trader's PositionSlot or LP slot.
